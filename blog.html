Countries Close to My Heart

"Well, when it comes to being quote-unquote International..."

At just 21 years young, I've somehow crammed my passport with stamps from 40 countries across 4 continents - from the frozen tundras of Russia to the chaotic motorbike streets of Vietnam. This isn't your polished Instagram travel blog. These are the raw, unfiltered stories of what travel actually looks like when you're young, and determined to see the world.

Vietnam

This Southeast Asian gem has gained prominence among world travelers in the past few years. Within its communist beginnings and colonial history lies a nation epitomizing Asia as a whole. Whether it's the capitals or the rainforests, the longest caves in the world, or the tourist-trodden bays, Vietnam is a budget-friendly and exciting destination to discover—whether you live right across the border or across the seven seas.

Planning Your Trip

1) Visa

The visa process for Vietnam is straightforward, as it offers visa-free entry or e-visa access to most nationalities. Be cautious of third-party websites charging high fees; apply directly through the official Vietnamese government website: Apply for a Vietnam e-Visa. The cost is around USD 25 per person.

2) Duration

The ideal length of stay depends on your budget, vacation days, and itinerary. A recommended duration is 15 days, allowing ample time to explore the country from north to south, beginning in one capital and ending in the other. Vietnam can be visited year-round, though summer months are hot and humid—perfect for enjoying Ha Long Bay.

3) Packing

Pack light for a warm and humid climate. Fun fact: many of your clothes are probably made in Vietnam! Instead of overpacking, consider buying high-quality, affordable clothing locally.

4) Flights

If you want to travel cheap, fly Viet Jet, it is as simple as that. They are the Wizz Air, the Frontier Airlines and the Spirit airlines of the East. Important: check the deals on their own website and nowhere else, some of the deals that they provide will make you rub your eyes in disbelief. However, if you do find comfort to be the most imnportant trait of your journey, Vietnam Airlines, Bamboo Airlines and Thai Airways provide great options with impeccable service. Authors Pick! - https://www.vietjetair.com/en

GEOGRAPHY AND HOT DESTINATIONS

It's best to travel the country lengthwise. Pick your starting point—either Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City (HCMC)—and end in the other, provided you have at least a fortnight to spare. If time is limited, focus on exploring destinations around either Hanoi or HCMC, as these cities lie at opposite ends of the country.

To help you plan, here are three route options based on your budget and available time.

15 DAYS OR MORE:

3 Days – Ho Chi Minh City

As an Indian, I like to call HCMC the "Bombay of Vietnam"—and rightly so. It's the country's bustling business hub, filled with skyscrapers, yet deeply rooted in history. The city played a pivotal role in Vietnam's independence from the French and its later liberation in the 1970s. Named after Ho Chi Minh, it's famous for the Cu Chi Tunnels, showcasing the brutal guerrilla warfare tactics used against the Americans.

Places of Interest:

a) Cu Chi Tunnels

A 75-minute drive northwest from Ho Chi Minh City transports you to one of Vietnam’s most haunting yet fascinating historical sites: the Cu Chi Tunnels. This sprawling 250-kilometer underground network—carved by hand into clay and laterite soil—served as a lifeline for Viet Cong guerrillas during the Vietnam War (1955–1975). More than just hiding spots, these tunnels were fully functional subterranean cities, complete with hospitals, weapon workshops, kitchens, and command centers.

Visitor Experience Today

Modern visitors can crawl through slightly widened tunnel sections (claustrophobia warning!), fire AK-47s at a shooting range (with live ammo), and watch archival propaganda films at the on-site museum. The adjacent Ben Duoc Memorial Temple honors 45,000 Vietnamese soldiers who died defending the tunnels.

Pro Tip:

Visit the Ben Dinh site for tourist-friendly tunnels, or Ben Duoc for a more authentic (but challenging) experience. Wear quick-dry clothing—the humidity hits 90% year-round!

War Echoes

Crater marks from B-52 bombs still pockmark the landscape, while rusted tank husks stand as grim monuments. Guides—often descendants of tunnel veterans—demonstrate how soldiers fashioned sandals from recycled tires and survived on cassava roots during sieges. The site forces reflection on war’s absurdity: U.S. forces once pumped 8,000 gallons of tear gas into tunnels... only for guerrillas to emerge days later, having dug new escape routes.

b) Bui Vien Street
After a day traversing the claustrophobic tunnels where soldiers once fought for survival, the sensory overload of Bui Vien Street delivers a visceral contrast. This 1.5-kilometer stretch in District 1 transforms at dusk into Ho Chi Minh City's pulsing "Western Quarter", where backpackers, expats, and adventurous locals collide in a neon-lit carnival of chaos.

Veterans of Pattaya's go-go bars or Phuket's ping pong shows will find Bui Vien refreshingly raw yet less predatory. The notable differences:

Pro Tip:
  • Timing: The street peaks between 10PM-2AM but bars serve until 4AM
  • Scams: Avoid "friendly" girls inviting you to "special bars"—they work on commission
  • Transport: Use Grab Bike (motorbike taxi) to escape—cars get gridlocked
  • Escape Pods: Duck into BiaCraft Artisan Ales for craft beer or Lush Nightclub for proper DJs

By midnight, Bui Vien becomes a living abstract painting: neon signs reflecting off spilled beer, the sizzle of banh trang nuong (Vietnamese pizza) griddles cutting through bass drops, and the occasional yelp of a tourist stepping on a discarded durian rind. It's not pretty—but like the Cu Chi Tunnels, it's authentically, unforgettably Vietnamese.

c) War Remnants Museum

No visit to Ho Chi Minh City is complete without confronting the brutal legacy of the Vietnam War at the War Remnants Museum. What begins as a typical museum visit quickly transforms into a visceral, emotional experience that demands at least 3-4 hours to fully absorb. The museum's exceptional audio tour (available in multiple languages) guides visitors through this comprehensive collection with heartbreaking narration from war survivors.

The Outdoor Display: Machinery of War

The courtyard hits you first - a chilling open-air graveyard of American military hardware:

  • UH-1 "Huey" helicopters - the iconic choppers that filled Vietnam's skies
  • M48 Patton tanks - their steel hulls still bearing shrapnel scars
  • F-5A fighter jets and BLU-82 "Daisy Cutter" bombs - the massive explosives that cleared jungle areas
  • Agent Orange spray tanks - the chemical weapons that poisoned generations

Indoor Exhibits: The Human Cost

Inside, the museum shifts from machines to human suffering through three floors of devastating exhibits:

  • "Requiem" Photo Gallery - Pulitzer-winning images of civilian casualties and wartime atrocities
  • Agent Orange Aftermath - jars of deformed fetuses and photos of children born with defects decades after the war
  • Tiger Cage Replicas - the barbaric prison cells used to torture Viet Cong prisoners
  • War Crimes Documentation - detailed accounts of the My Lai Massacre and other atrocities
Visitor's Note:

The museum presents a distinctly Vietnamese perspective on the war. Some displays are graphic and emotionally overwhelming - we observed many visitors needing to take breaks in the courtyard. The audio tour (100,000 VND ~ $4) is essential for understanding context beyond the visual displays.

Tip: Visit in the morning when you're fresh - the heavy content can be exhausting. The rooftop cafe provides a quiet space to reflect afterward.
